Fixing a Broken Team Before Building a Better Product

How redefining collaboration norms and rebuilding cross-functional relationships at a Fortune 500 bank helped deliver a 3,000-screen platform rebuild 7 months ahead of schedule.

The Challenge

When I joined this engagement, the team had a problem that no roadmap was going to fix. A massive front-end rebuild was on the table — over 3,000 screens across web and Android platforms — and the projected timeline was 17 months. That number alone should have been a red flag. But the deeper issue wasn't scope. It was that the team had no real way of working together.

Collaboration was broken. Stakeholders were siloed. Engineers, designers, and business partners operated in parallel but rarely in sync. There were no shared rituals, no alignment sessions, no consistent way to surface blockers or decisions before they became emergencies. Everyone was busy, but the work wasn't moving. The dysfunction was quiet — no one was fighting — but the friction was everywhere, and it was costing the project time before a single sprint had begun.

The most impactful product decision wasn't a feature or a framework. It was deciding to fix how the team worked together before writing a single line of code.

The Approach

Before touching the product work, I focused on the operating model. I started by mapping the existing interaction patterns — who was talking to whom, how often, and about what. What I found was a web of informal, inconsistent communication. Decisions were being made in hallways or not made at all. Stakeholders felt out of the loop. Engineers were building against requirements that hadn't been properly validated. Designers were handing off work into a void.

I introduced a set of structured collaboration rituals that most teams take for granted but this one had never formalized. Regular stakeholder syncs with clear agendas. Joint customer journey mapping sessions that got engineers and business partners looking at the same problems at the same time. Brainstorming sessions with defined outputs rather than open-ended conversations that went nowhere. A shared decision log so nothing fell through the cracks.

The goal wasn't to add process for its own sake. It was to create shared context. When everyone understands not just what they're building but why — and when the people making decisions are talking to the people executing them — the work accelerates naturally. Blockers surface earlier. Rework drops. Trust builds. The cross-functional relationships that had been transactional became genuinely collaborative.

The Outcome

A project projected to take 17 months was delivered in 10. That's not a small efficiency gain — it's a fundamentally different outcome. The 3,000-screen rebuild shipped across both web and Android platforms, and time to market was reduced by 40%.

The metrics matter, but the more important story is what changed underneath them. By the end of the engagement, the team had a working model they owned. Stakeholders knew what was happening and when. Engineers had the context they needed to make good decisions in the moment. The collaboration norms we established didn't just help deliver this project — they became the foundation for how the team would work going forward.

It's a reminder that the best thing a product leader can sometimes do is resist the urge to jump straight into the product, and instead spend time making sure the humans building it are set up to succeed.
