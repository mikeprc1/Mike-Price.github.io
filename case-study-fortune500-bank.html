Fixing a Broken Team Before Building a Better Product

How redefining collaboration norms and rebuilding cross-functional relationships at a Fortune 100 bank helped deliver a 3,000-screen platform rebuild 7 months ahead of schedule.

The Challenge

When I joined this engagement, the team had a problem that no roadmap was going to fix. A massive front-end rebuild was on the table. Over 3,000 screens across web and Android platforms. The projected timeline was 17 months. That was an aggressive delivery timeline, but the bigger problem was that the team had no real way of working together.

Collaboration was broken. Stakeholders were siloed. Engineers, designers, and business partners operated in parallel but rarely in sync. There were no alignment, no consistent way to surface blockers or decisions before they became emergencies, and most telling, no sense of shared accountability. Everyone was busy, but the work wasn't moving. Frankly, it was dysfunctional. Our biggest problems were internal. We couldn't solve the customer problem without solving our own first.

The most impactful product decision was fixing how the team worked together before writing a single line of code.

The Approach

Before touching the product work, I focused on the operating model. I started by mapping the existing interaction patterns. Who was talking to whom, how often, and about what. What I found was a web of informal, inconsistent communication. We had multiple people circling the same areas, but never working together. Yet despite everyone being busy, engineers were building against requirements that nobody in the Product organization understood, let alone wrote. Designers hadn't even brainstormed experiences, yet engineers were building them. Honestly, to this day, I'm still not sure how it ever got that bad

I introduced a set of structured collaboration rituals that most teams at most companies take for granted but this one had never formalized any of it. We started having regular stakeholder syncs with clear agendas. Joint customer journey mapping sessions that got engineers and business partners looking at the same problems at the same time. Brainstorming sessions with defined outputs rather than open-ended conversations that went nowhere. A shared decision log so everything was traceable and accountability was shared.

The Outcome

A product modernization projected to take 17 months was delivered in 10. The 3,000-screen rebuild shipped across both web and Android platforms, and time to market was reduced by 40%.

The metrics matter, but the more important story is what changed underneath them. By the end of the engagement, the team had a working model they owned. Stakeholders knew what was happening and when. Engineers had the context they needed to make good decisions in the moment. The collaboration norms we established became the foundation for how the team would work going forward.

It's a reminder that the best thing a product leader can sometimes do is resist the urge to jump straight into the product, and instead spend time making sure the humans building it are set up to succeed. Sometimes the products you build are only as strong as the team behind them.
