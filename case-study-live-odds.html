What Building a Sports Betting App Taught Me About AI-Assisted Development

The honest lessons from vibe-coding a real production app — from first prompt to pre-production meltdown — and what it taught me about the gap between what AI can start and what humans still need to finish.

The Idea

The tools that exist for serious sports bettors are either painful to use or painfully expensive. The good ones run $250 a month on the low end. I wanted to build something better — an app that aggregates live odds across 1,000+ global sportsbooks in real time and uses logic to surface the three bet types that sharp bettors actually look for: arbitrage opportunities, positive expected value bets, and negative correlation plays.

I also wanted to build it myself. Not because I'm an engineer — I'm not — but because I was genuinely curious what a product manager with a clear vision and access to modern AI coding tools could actually ship. The answer turned out to be: a lot, and also, it's complicated.

The first version took a plain English idea to a working prototype faster than anything I'd ever seen. The second version nearly broke everything we'd built.

The Approach

The early stages were genuinely remarkable. I described what I wanted in plain English, fed it to an AI coding agent, and watched a working front-end prototype materialize faster than any design-to-dev handoff I'd ever managed in a professional setting. The gap between idea and first working version had never felt smaller.

But iteration was a different story entirely. The jump from first prototype to something precise enough to hand back to an AI agent for the next build phase is a problem nobody talks about honestly enough. A human developer can work from a rough brief and ask clarifying questions. An AI agent works from what you give it — and if what you give it is ambiguous, it will make decisions you didn't intend and sometimes quietly undo the ones you did.

The most instructive moment came during a period of frequent changes. I'd ask the agent to make one modification, and it would make it — while also reversing something it had done in the previous session. There was no malice in it. The agent simply had no reliable memory of every decision it had made across a long build. It was filling in gaps with its best judgment, and its best judgment didn't always match mine.

The Lessons

Testing exposed a different class of problem. During pre-production, we were flipping between dummy data for AI calls and real production APIs — a manual toggle we'd added in development to prevent live sandbox calls hitting the production environment. When it came time to test in pre-production, the agent had distributed that toggle throughout the codebase in ways neither of us had fully tracked. Cleaning it up was significantly harder than putting it in had been.

The most memorable moment was simpler: I asked the agent to hide the admin panel from the production-facing app until we had proper user permissions built out. It obliged — and came up with a solution on its own. To access the admin panel, you had to click a specific menu item five times in sequence. Clever, actually. The problem was that a week later, neither the agent nor I could remember where it had hidden the trigger. We eventually found it, but it was a useful reminder that AI-generated solutions optimized for the immediate problem don't always account for the humans who have to maintain them.

What I came away with is a sharper mental model for how AI-assisted development actually works in practice. The first mile is extraordinary. The middle miles require a level of precision and documentation discipline that most people underestimate. The tooling is improving rapidly — but right now, the biggest unlock isn't prompting better. It's knowing exactly what you want before you ask, and building systems to track what the agent has done so you can course-correct without losing ground. That's a product management problem as much as a technical one.
