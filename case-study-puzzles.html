Growing Puzzle Sales One Piece at a Time

How some minor tweaks and a sub-brand creation increased total sales by 15% and repeat sales by 12% in 30 days.

The Challenge

Sales were down. Their core business was selling specialty puzzles to a niche customer base. That niche market was shrinking and sales were down across the board. This family-owned business needed to attract new customers and they needed to do it fast.

A seemingly small UI change; adding a new filter to their product search; accounted for nearly 30% of searches and had 3x the standard sales conversion rate.

The Approach

We created specific customer segments that had unique challenges and objectives. We needed a way to differentiate the person who buys a puzzle once a year for their gransdson's birthday from the avid puzzle enthusiast that has a house filled with them. Presumably, different segments have different methods of shopping for puzzles. One probably goes to target and picks one out based on price. The other probably narrows their search based on theme and reads every word on the packaging before deciding to buy.

Once we knew which segment we wanted to target, we set up a series of dozens of paid interviews via Upwork. The vetting process was challenging. As we went through each interview, I would feed a trained AI agent the transcript from the recorded interview. It would then go to an opportunity board we created in Figma and populate each column with the appropriate response. It would also identify any unique responses that we received and add a note to track it for possible further investigation.

After all the interviews we complete, I then had another AI agent synthesize a review deck that laid out all of the answers to our key questions, chart the key metrics we were investigating and lay out a list of recommendations to meet our goals. I touched them up and prioritized them in by relevance, impact and size of effort to execute and presented them back to owners of the business.

The Outcome

I made three key recommendations. One was a small UI change. There was a specific piece of information avid puzzle enthusiasts were interested in knowing when purchasing a puzzle. Very few puzzles have it to them in their product listings. 52% of customers mentioned in their interviews. They could add it to every product listing and add it as a sorting tool and search filter. They made these changes and it accounted for nearly 30% of searches and had 3x the standard sales conversion rate.

The second recommendation was establishing a themed sub-brand series of puzzles that would resonate with customers. This creates familiarity and brand recognition with customers and lead to more repeat sales. Through establishing this sub-brand and then surfacing other puzzles within the sub-brand to those same customers, repeat sales increased by 12% the first month it was live.

The third and final recommendation came from an entirely different style of puzzle game that we discovered was ballooning in popularity. It was a completely new and different genre of puzzle that the company had never created before, but was massively popular with people aged 25 to 35. Unfortunately, the effort to pursue the opportunity, along with the risk it would introduce was too great and they decided not to implement this recommendation.
